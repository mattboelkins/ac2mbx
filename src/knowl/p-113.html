Note particularly that the instantaneous rate of change at \(x = a\) is the limit of the average rate of change on \([a,a+h]\) as \(h \to 0\). \item Provided the derivative \(f'(a)\) exists, its value tells us the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) at \(x = a\), which geometrically is the slope of the tangent line to the curve \(y = f(x)\) at the point \((a,f(a))\). We even say that \(f'(a)\) is the slope of the curve \(y = f(x)\) at the point \((a,f(a))\). \item Limits are the link between average rate of change and instantaneous rate of change: they allow us to move from the rate of change over an interval to the rate of change at a single point.
