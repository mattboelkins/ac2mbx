Item 11
First note that the terms \(\frac{1}{\ln(k)}\) decrease to \(0\), so the Alternating Series Test shows that the alternating series \(\ds \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k}{\ln(k)}\) converges. Let \(S = \ds \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k}{\ln(k)}\) and let \(S_n\) be the \(n\)th partial sum of this series. Recall that \[ |S_n - S| \lt |S_n - S_{n+1}| = a_{n+1} \] where \(a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{\ln(n+1)}\). If we can find a value of \(n\) so that \(a_{n+1} \lt 0.001\), then \(|S_n - S| \lt 0.001\) as desired. Now \begin{align*} a_{n+1} \amp \lt 0.001\\ \frac{1}{\ln(n+1)} \amp \lt 0.001\\ \ln(n+1) \amp > 1000\\ n+1 \amp > e^{1000}\\ n \amp > e^{1000}-1. \end{align*} So if we choose \(n = e^{1000}\), then \(S_n\) approximates the sum of the series to within 0.001. Notice that \(e^{1000} \approx 1.97 \times 10^{434}\) is a very large number. This shows that the series \(\ds \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^k}{\ln(k)}\) converges very slowly.
