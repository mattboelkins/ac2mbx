We can continue approximating \(e\) with polynomials of larger degree whose higher derivatives agree with those of \(f\) at 0. This turns out to make the polynomials fit the graph of \(f\) better for more values of \(x\) around 0. For example, let \(P_3(x) = 1+x+\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^3}{6}\). Show that \(P_3(0) = f(0)\), \(P'_3(0) = f'(0)\), \(P''_3(0) = f''(0)\), and \(P'''_3(0) = f'''(0)\). Use \(P_3(x)\) to approximate \(e\) in a way similar to how you did so with \(P_2(x)\) above. Solution

The derivatives of \(P_3\) and \(f\) are

\begin{align*} P_3(x) \amp = 1+x+\frac{x^2}{2}+\frac{x^3}{6} \amp f(x) \amp = e^x\\ P'_3(x) \amp = 1 + x +\frac{x^2}{2} \amp f'(x) \amp = e^x\\ P''_3(x) \amp = 1+x \amp f''(x) \amp = e^x\\ P'''_3(x) \amp = 1 \amp f'''(x) \amp = e^x, \end{align*}

and so the derivatives of \(P_2\) and \(f\) evaluated at 0 are

\begin{align*} P_3(0) \amp = 1 \amp f(0) \amp = e^0 = 1\\ P'_3(0) \amp = 1 \amp f'(0) \amp = e^0 = 1\\ P''_3(0) \amp = 1 \amp f''(0) \amp = e^0 = 1\\ P'''_3(0) \amp = 1 \amp f'''(0) \amp = e^0 = 1. \end{align*}

Then

\[ e = e^1 \approx P_3(1) = 1 + 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{8}{3} \approx 2.67. \]
