Solution 8.3.3.1
  1. Notice that the \(n\)th partial sum of the series \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k}\) is equal to the left hand Riemann sum of \(f(x)\) on the interval \([1,n]\).
  2. Since \(f\) is a decreasing function, it follows that \[ \ds \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{k} > \int_{1}^{n} \frac{1}{x} \ dx. \]
  3. Letting Since \(f\) is decreasing, the improper integral \(\ds \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x} \ dx\) is smaller than the limit of the Riemann sums as \(n\) goes to infinity. So we wind up with a comparison between the series \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}\) and the improper integral: \[ \ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k} > \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x} \ dx. \]
  4. We can evaluate the improper integral as follows: \begin{align*} \int_{1}^{\infty} f(x) \ dx \amp = \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_{1}^{t} \frac{1}{x} \ dx\\ \amp = \lim_{t \to \infty} \ln(x) |_{1}^{t}\\ \amp = \lim_{t \to \infty} \left(\ln(t) - \ln(1) \right)\\ \amp = \infty. \end{align*} Since the value of the series \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k}\) exceeds the value of the infinite improper integral, we must conclude that the series \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k}\) diverges.
