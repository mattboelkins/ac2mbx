Exercise 8.3.15

Consider the series defined by

\begin{equation} \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{2^k}{3^k-k}. \label{eq_8.3_ratio_example}\tag{8.3.4}\end{equation}

This series is not a geometric series, but this activity will illustrate how we might compare this series to a geometric one. Recall that a series \(\sum a_k\) is geometric if the ratio \(\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\) is always the same. For the series in (8.3.4), note that \(a_k = \frac{2^k}{3^k-k}\).

  1. To see if \(\sum \frac{2^k}{3^k-k}\) is comparable to a geometric series, we analyze the ratios of successive terms in the series. Complete Table 8.3.16, listing your calculations to at least 8 decimal places.
  2. Based on your calculations in Table 8.3.16, what can we say about the ratio \(\frac{a_{k+1}}{a_k}\) if \(k\) is large?
  3. Do you agree or disagree with the statement: “the series \(\sum \frac{2^k}{3^k-k}\) is approximately geometric when \(k\) is large”? If not, why not? If so, do you think the series \(\sum \frac{2^k}{3^k-k}\) converges or diverges? Explain.
