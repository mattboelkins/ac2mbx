Item 3
We know that \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\) is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 2 > 1\) and so is a convergent series. Since \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2} = -\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\), the series \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} -\frac{1}{k^2}\) also converges. Since the terms in the series \[ 1 - \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{16} + \frac{1}{25} + \frac{1}{36} - \frac{1}{49} - \frac{1}{64} - \frac{1}{81} - \frac{1}{100} + \cdots \] converge to 0 and the series itself is bounded between two convergent series, we should expect this series to converge to some finite number between \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} -\frac{1}{k^2}\) and \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^2}\).
