Item 1
Consider the series \[ \sum \frac{1}{k^2} \hspace{0.5in} \text{ and } \hspace{0.5in} \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}. \] We know that the series \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) is a \(p\)-series with \(p = 2 > 1\) and so \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) converges. In this part of the exercise we will see how to use information about \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) to determine information about \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\). Let \(a_k = \frac{1}{k^2}\) and \(b_k = \frac{1}{k^2+k}\).
  • Let \(S_n\) be the \(n\)th partial sum of \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) and \(T_n\) the \(n\)th partial sum of \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\). Which is larger, \(S_1\) or \(T_1\)? Why? Solution

    Since \(S_1 = 1\) and \(T_1 = \frac{1}{2}\), we see that \(S_1 > T_1\).

  • Recall that \[ S_2 = S_1 + a_2 \hspace{0.5in} \text{ and } \hspace{0.5in} T_2 = T_1 + b_2. \] Which is larger, \(a_2\) or \(b_2\)? Based on that answer, which is larger, \(S_2\) or \(T_2\)? Solution

    Since \(a_2 = \frac{1}{4}\) and \(b_2 = \frac{1}{6}\), it is the case that \(a_2 > b_2\). We already know that \(S_1 > T_1\), so adding a larger number to \(S_1\) than we add to \(T_1\) makes \(S_2 > T_2\).

  • Recall that \[ S_3 = S_2 + a_3 \hspace{0.5in} \text{ and } \hspace{0.5in} T_3 = T_2 + b_3. \] Which is larger, \(a_3\) or \(b_3\)? Based on that answer, which is larger, \(S_3\) or \(T_3\)? Solution

    Since \(a_3 = \frac{1}{9}\) and \(b_3 = \frac{1}{12}\), it is the case that \(a_3 > b_3\). We already know that \(S_2 > T_2\), so adding a larger number to \(S_2\) than we add to \(T_2\) makes \(S_3 > T_3\).

  • Which is larger, \(a_n\) or \(b_n\)? Explain. Based on that answer, which is larger, \(S_n\) or \(T_n\)? Solution

    Since \(n^2+n > n^2\) when \(n\) is positive, we have \(\frac{1}{n^2} > \frac{1}{n^2+n}\) or \(a_n > b_n\). So at each step, we are adding larger numbers to the partial sums of \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) than we are to the partial sums of \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\), we conclude that \(S_n > T_n\).

  • Based on your response to the previous part of this exercise, what relationship do you expect there to be between \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) and \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\)? Do you expect \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\) to converge or diverge? Why? Solution

    Since \(S_n > T_n\), we should expect \( \lim_{n \to \infty} S_n > \lim_{n \to \infty} T_n\) or

    \[ \sum \frac{1}{k^2} > \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}. \]

    Since \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) is a \(p\)-series with \(p=2 > 1\), we know that \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) converges (and is finite). Now \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\) is a sum of positive terms and is therefore positive, and is also less than a finite number (\( \sum \frac{1}{k^2}\)), so we expect that \( \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\) converges as well.
