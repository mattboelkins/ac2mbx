Solution 4.3.2.1
  1. Because \(y = 3x\) and the \(x\)-axis bound a triangle with base of length 1 and height \(3\) on the interval \([0,1]\), it follows that \[ \ds \int_0^1 3x \, dx = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot 3 = \frac{3}{2}. \]
  2. For \(\ds \int_{-1}^4 (2-2x) \, dx\), we first sketch the region bounded by the function, as shown below. The line creates two triangles, one with area \(A_1 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 4 = 4\) and the other with area \(A_2 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 3 \cdot 6 = 9\). Since the latter area corresponds to a region below the \(x\)-axis, we associate a negative sign to it, and hence find that \[ \int_{-1}^4 (2-2x) \, dx = A_1 - A_2 = 4 - 9 = -5. \]
  3. For \(\ds \int_{-1}^1 \sqrt{1-x^2} \, dx\), we simply observe that this function is the top half of a circle of radius 1, and thus the bounded region is a semicircle of radius 1, thus having an area of \(\frac{\pi}{2}\). Therefore, \[ \ds \int_{-1}^1 \sqrt{1-x^2} \, dx = \frac{\pi}{2}. \]
  4. Finally, for \(\ds \int_{-3}^4 g(x) \, dx\), where \(g\) is the function pictured in FigureÂ 4.3.8, we consider the function on seven consecutive subintervals of length 1. Observe that on \([-3,-2]\), the bounded area is \(\frac{1}{2}\). On \([-2,-1]\), the area is \(\frac{1}{4} \pi\). Similarly, on the next five subintervals of length 1, the areas bounded are respectively \(\frac{1}{2}\), \(1\), \(\frac{1}{2}\), \(\frac{1}{4} \pi\), and \(\frac{1}{4} \pi\). Thus, the value of the integral is \[ \int_{-3}^4 g(x) \, dx = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\pi}{4} - \frac{1}{2} - 1 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{3\pi}{4} - \frac{3}{2}, \] which is approximately 0.8562.
