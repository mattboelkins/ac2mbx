Item 3
Assume \(p \lt 1\). Then \(1-p > 0\) and so \[ \lim_{x \to \infty} x^{-p+1} = \infty. \] Thus, \begin{align*} \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \ dx \amp = \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_{1}^{t} \frac{1}{x^p} \ dx\\ \amp = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{x^{-p+1}}{-p+1} \biggm|_{1}^{t}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{1-p} \lim_{t \to \infty} \left(t^{-p+1} - 1 \right)\\ \amp = \infty. \end{align*} So the improper integral \( \int_1^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \ dx\) diverges when \(p \lt 1\). The Integral Test then shows that the series \( \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k^p}\) also diverges when \(p \lt 1\).
